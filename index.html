The Changing Face of Catholicism in the United States

By Colleen Crowley

In the United States, Catholicism started out as the religion of immigrants. As floods of newcomers arrived on these shores in the 19th century, they initially faced resistance from native-born Americans. The Catholic population continued to grow and became one of the largest religious groups in the nation. However, recent studies have shown that the church is in danger of losing many of its followers. Now, the survival of the largest Christian denomination in the country depends upon immigrants once more.

A Brief History

During the infancy of the United States, almost everyone practiced one of the various denominations of Protestantism. A small Roman Catholic population existed, mainly in the Maryland area, and in 1789, the Diocese of Baltimore became the first Catholic diocese in the nation.In the mid 19th century, the wave of Irish and German Catholic immigrants quickly began changing the religious makeup of the country. What was once a mostly Protestant nation was now home to millions of Catholics, mostly in the urban centers of the northeast. The arrival of Southern and Eastern European Catholics, like Italians and Poles, continued to boost the population and by the turn of the century, nearly 1/6 of the population was Catholic

With the arrival of Roman Catholic immigrants came anti-Catholic sentiment, first in the form of the Know Nothing movement in the 1850’s and then in the second rise of the Ku Klux Klan in 1915. However, by the middle of the 20th century, anti-Catholic sentiment died down and by 1960, the United States had its first (and only) Roman Catholic president.


Now, nearly 25 percent of the United States population is Catholic. However, that could easily change. Whether it is due to scandals, secularism or a genuine loss of faith, the Catholic Church in the United States is seeing the faithful leave in droves.

In the Moment

As American society moves closer towards a more secular lifestyle, many religions, including Catholicsm, have seen a decrease in their ranks. Although the Catholic community in the United States is growing from a numbers standpoint, the overall percentage of americans who practice the religion has dropped significantly.According to the Center for Applied Research in the Apostolate (CARA), the number of parish-connected Catholics has grown from 47.9 million in 1970 to 68.5 million in 2017. However, that increase doesn’t take into account the general population growth of the country in the last half century. As a result of that boom, the Catholic community, which once comprised 33.3 percent of the US population in 1965, has decreased to 25.1 percent of the population in 2010, a net loss of 8.2 percent, according to The Washington Post.

There are several factors for this decrease in the overall percentage of practicing Catholics in the country. Firstly, the number of former Catholics is on the rise, from 7.5 million in 1975 to 30 million in 2017 (CARA). Shifting cultural attitudes towards homosexuality, women’s productive rights, extra marital sexual activity and other issues have turned many people, especially younger adults, away from an institution that many view as “behind the times.”


Secondly, according to CARA, the number of baptisms year-over-year has plummeted, from 1.089 million in 1970 to 660,367 in 2017. Thirdly, the Catholic population as a whole is getting older. A recent National Geographic report showed that from 1987 to 2014, the percent of Catholics aged 50+ increased from 32 percent to 50 percent. Additionally, a Pew Research Center study revealed that the median age for a Catholic person has increased, from 45 years old in 2007 to 49 years old in 2014.

Looking Forward

Despite the dismal numbers, there is hope for the Catholic Church in the United States, and it is once again due to the faith of immigrants. The number of foreign-born Catholic adults in the U.S. is on the rise, from 4.7 million in 1975 to 16.9 million in 2017, according to CARA. Many of these individuals are Hispanic; National Geographic reports that the Hispanic population within the U.S. Catholic Church had risen from just 10 percent of adherents in 1987 to 34 percent in 2014.

In fact, the Catholic Church in the U.S. is surprisingly diverse. From 2007 to 2014, the percentage of non-white Catholics in the US has increased from 35 percent to 41 percent of the total Catholic population, according to the Pew Research Center study. That makes Catholicism the third most diverse Christian group (percentage-wise) in the country, after historically black Protestants and Jehovah’s Witness.

And so, as times change, so will the Catholic Church in America. Immigrants make up the fabric of the United States, and they make up the soul of the nation’s Catholic Church. They always have.
